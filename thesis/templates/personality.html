Carl Gustav Jung was a Swiss psychiatrist whose work was heavily influenced by psychoanalysis. He worked on research projects with Sigmund Freud and was significantly affected by him. In the end, though, Jung rejected a lot of Freud's ideas. Jung is well recognized for his work on the human mind, dream analysis, and personality.

The psychological types hypothesis developed by Carl G. Jung [Jung, 1971] states that persons may be classified based on their preferred general attitude: Extraverted (E) vs. Introverted (I), which of the two perceptual functions they prefer: Sensing (S) vs. Intuition (N), and which of the two judgment duties they prefer to perform: Thinking (T) vs. Feeling (F). The three regions of choice that Jung identified are contrasts (i.e. bipolar dimensions where each pole represents a different preference). Jung also suggested that one of the four functions listed above—either a function of perception or a function of judgment—is prominent in a person. A scholar and practitioner of Jung's theory named Isabel Briggs Myers argued that a fourth dichotomy determining personality type be the judging-perceiving connection [Briggs Myers, 1980]: Judging (J) vs. Perceiving (P).

1. Extraversion – Introversion, denotes a person's energy expression's source and direction. A source of energy for an extravert is mostly found in the outside world, whereas a source of energy for an introvert is primarily found within.

2. Sensing – Intuition, describes the way that a person processes information. By sensing, a person signifies that he or she primarily accepts information that comes from the outside environment. When someone has intuition, they tend to believe information they get from their inner or imaginary world.

3. Thinking – Feeling, indicates a person's information processing style. Thinking implies that a person primarily uses reasoning to reach a choice. Feeling denotes that a person often bases decisions on their feelings, i.e., what they believe they should do.

4. Judging – Perceiving, demonstrates how a person uses the knowledge that he or she has absorbed. Judging entails planning every aspect of one's life and, generally speaking, adhering to one's plans. Perceiving indicates a person's propensity to improvise and consider other possibilities.

There are 16 different combinations, or personality types, that result from all possible permutations of preferences in the four dichotomies mentioned above. These combinations indicate which of the two poles in each of the four dichotomies dominates in an individual, defining the 16 different personality types.
